On humans’ fantasy and prediction about technology

November 03 2017
6 min read

Originally written on: 11 Dec 2014, 12:35 AM

What conjures up in your mind when I talk about Harry Potter’s pensieve, where one can store memories. Memories are stored in some kind of a silver sink with silken threads taken out of your mind, which represents each memory. Or about a speaking tree. Where one can go and talk to the tree to store one’s memories, and the tree speaks back by relieving one’s past memory. I don’t exactly know what might conjure up in your mind, but I’m pretty sure whatever visual you have in your mind, it is in the past world. In an ancient world. Okay, maybe not ancient, but certainly not in the current age of digital technology with glass panels in you hands and living in glass buildings.

Why is that?

If we look at history and imagination of people in the past, we see very interesting things in terms of magic or fantasy. I would go further and argue that prediction about the future was also very similar. The common thing about them, I believe, is - technology.

But the technologies that they imagined were all — for lack of a better word, I know its a little-bit hand-wavy, I have not set any context or definition, but here you go — organic. If we look at today’s machines, they don’t seem to be what our ancestors had imagined our advanced technology or magic/fantasy might look like.

They would have imagined some kind of silver sink with silken threads in them to store memories. Akin to the pensieve in Harry Potter. Or maybe some kind of old aged tree to which you can talk to save and retrieve memories. But today we capture our memories through digital machines and upload them on the global-scape of information aka the Internet; or store them away in our clunky yet fragile external hard disks.

Who would have thought giant machine spitting out smoke and steam with revolving gears much larger than men, could do miracles. People in the past always thought, riding on the broomstick was the way to fly. Or maybe some magic carpet. If these seem to far fetched into fantasy, maybe the critical thinkers and tinkerers of the olden days had thought about much realistic machines which could, say, fly or run on the ground.

But technologists since the ancient times have brought in technology but always in a crude way. The motto was make the thing work and not really preconcieve the consequences of the technology. As a result we as a race have developed a natural tendency to develop new technolgies in that way. Over the period of centuries this has impacted the environment we live in.

Technology which has been widely accepted, i.e. things after electricity, has been disruptive in a negative way. Obviously the concept or the scientific discovery behind these technologies is sheer breathtaking and one can only wonder in such astonishing studies and findings. But the method they have been impacting our lives is probably inorganic. Could we have built technolgies that were more organic and hence it could fall into the natural cycle of any biosphere? But then we have to figure out what do we mean by organic technology and inorganic technology. Even if we postpone defining the terms, can’t we say that when humans discovered they could make bricks out of clay and put them together to build stronger houses, or they could make stronger and even more effective weapons with use of a metal instead of stones which occur naturally in the Earth’s crust albeit you have to excavate it, or they could make something like fire out of almost nothing and then use it to cook food to get rid of various diseaeses of the food or use it to stay warm etc, that they were discovering organic technolgies? So where can we draw line saying that humans switched from organic technology to inorganic technology? Can we say that the industrial revolution flipped the tables around? What would we mean by that? Do we mean smoke producing steam engines killed the organic technology development or the noise making huge machines in factory? So then by that we would mean machines when/which started creating environmental pollution were the first ones to be inorganic. But then who would know that a smoke creating steam engine could be inorganic. People were already burining huge fires to fill their needs. There were already smoke pollution happening since centuries. We can pull out probably even more examples to show that what we thik as a industrial age technology and that being inorganic we can correspondingly find technologies from the pre-industrial era to be inorganic as well. So we can see the line between what we are trying to call organic/inorganic technology is blurred. Technologies what we like to think as inorganic have been there for centuries without being disruptive to the environment, and equally organic technologies have existed for centuries and continues to be developed even now. We have still not grounded on what we mean by organic technology and what we mean by inorganic technology. Before we do that and delve into deeper discussion of a timeline of important technologies over the centuries and classifying them as being organic/inorganic (I’d like to do that), I’d like to present another idea.

What if the type of technology(organic/inorganic) is not the problem, but the scale of its use is. What I mean by that is burning of fire is a technology which can have its tremendous benefits - like cooking food to make it safe from germs, or staying warm and fend off animals in night, it does have its share of being adverse on the environment. It produces carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide and other gases which have a negative effect on the environment. Burning fire for centuries did not affect the environment, but now even burning a small fire in a remote hill station on a cold night (which is essential for survival) contributes its fair share of air pollution to our current environment; leaving out the huge number of factories, sheer number of autmobiles etc. So does classifying technologies into being organic/inorganic really make sense to understand what impact it has on our environment? Or do we concentrate more on the scale of its use, which actually seem to contribute to the current problem we percieve we are having with technology? So to understand the problem how technology has been impacting our environment in a negative way, we have conlcuded there are two options to investigate to:

  1. The sheer scale of the uses of technology. (too much population - everybody wants a car - and everyone has to drive to office at 9am - probably causing massive air pollution at those very timings. [probably not only the scale but our current way of living and the way society functions also contributes to negative impact of the environment - think about everybody wants a car and everybody has to get to office at 9am])

  2. the type of technologies - organic and inorganic. organic: good, recycles itself, no impact on nature; inorganic: bad, byproducts and un-recyclable waste, and have negative impact on nature.

Next task is to think, dig deep into these two points.
